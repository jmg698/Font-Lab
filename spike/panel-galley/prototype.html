ESSAY

Choosing type should feel like reading, not guessing

By Dana Whitfield · 6 min read

We used to pick fonts by squinting at a dropdown and hoping. The trouble is that a typeface only tells the truth once it’s set in real sentences, at real sizes, next to the rest of your interface. A specimen sheet can flatter anything.

That is why testing pairings in situ matters. Swap the display face and the headline’s whole posture changes; swap the body and the reading rhythm shifts under your feet. The best decisions come from comparing candidates against what already ships.

“Type is the interface. Everything else is arrangement.”

Three roles carry most of the weight in a reading experience:

DISPLAY
headlines, mastheads, and moments that set a tone
BODY
the long middle where readers actually live
MONO
code, labels, timestamps, and quiet machinery
font-family: var(--display);  /* headlines */
font-family: var(--body);     /* paragraphs */
font-family: var(--mono);     /* this block */

A good tool keeps you honest: it renders the candidate on your page, tells you exactly what changed, and gets out of the way while you read. The pick should be the easy part.

What the eye checks first

Color of the text block at arm’s length. The fit of the headline against the measure. Whether numerals sit still in a table. Whether italics whisper or shout. None of this appears in a font menu; all of it appears on your own page.
